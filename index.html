The Quiet Discipline of Hardware Leadership

Interview and research by Jiwon Lee and Amanda Valverde

A collaborative microsite reflection on how hardware leadership turns ambition into buildable, trustworthy products through communication, feasibility, responsibility, and human rituals that keep a team moving.

Open the sections ↓ Read the synthesis ↗

01 · Context

Leadership as the work of making ideas survive reality.

For this interview, Jiwon Lee and Amanda Valverde spoke with Joshua Markell, who leads hardware at Meter, the networking company where Jiwon has been interning for the past four months.

Interview setting

Jiwon Lee and Amanda Valverde conducted the interview in person at the Meter office. The conversation lasted about an hour, and Jiwon’s work inside the hardware team gave the research a close observational lens on Joshua’s communication, feedback, and decision-making.

Company context

Meter builds enterprise-grade internet infrastructure through a full-stack approach that combines hardware, software, and operations. In 2025, Meter publicly announced nine new hardware platforms across access points, switches, firewalls, and gateways, describing them as a cohesive system designed to perform better, last longer, and integrate into Meter’s singular network stack.

Research vantage point

Jiwon’s internship work has been more involved with early ideation, packaging, graphics, and the visual side of product development, while Joshua manages the larger hardware production process. As research partners, Jiwon and Amanda used those observations alongside the interview to examine how his leadership shapes product decisions and team culture.

First signal

Jiwon remembered Joshua from her internship interview because he asked what she thought about the Cybertruck. It felt casual, but it also tested how she observes design, forms an opinion, and explains her reasoning.

02 · Collapsible interview sections

Ten lessons from a hardware leader.

Each section can be read in two layers: the visible summary and insight first, then the full reflection when opened.

01

ExecutionSuppliersResponsibility

Joshua’s answer to what keeps him up at night was grounded: supplier calls, pricing negotiation, and the constant work of bringing an idea into real life.

Key insightA design leader protects the concept while making sure it can survive production.

That answer reminded us that hardware leadership is not only about vision or aesthetics. A physical product has to pass through suppliers, manufacturing partners, cost constraints, timelines, quality checks, and production realities. Unlike digital design, hardware cannot always be revised instantly after launch. Every decision has physical, financial, and operational consequences.

Meter’s new hardware stack makes that challenge even more complex. The company has publicly described the redesign as thousands of deliberate choices, including port placement, airflow, fan behavior, and power management. Joshua’s role is connected to all of these layers. He is not just thinking about what a product should look like. He is thinking about whether it can be built, how much it will cost, how it will perform, how it will be deployed, and how it will represent Meter as a company.

This helped us understand the difference between being a designer and being a design leader. A designer may focus deeply on the quality of a concept. A leader has to protect the concept while also making sure it can survive reality.

02

AmbitionFeasibilityTranslation

Joshua described persuasion, honesty, and transparent communication as the daily work of balancing company ambition with hardware production realities.

Key insightConstraints become useful when leaders communicate them without killing momentum.

This answer stood out because it made leadership feel less like authority and more like translation. A hardware leader has to translate between different types of urgency. The company wants to move quickly. The CEO may see possibility and momentum. Engineers and suppliers may see constraints, risk, and cost. A leader has to communicate between those worlds without becoming negative or defensive.

Joshua also mentioned that being transparent can be hard when he does not always have access to every piece of information. That showed us that leaders also operate with incomplete knowledge. They are expected to make clear decisions, but they do not always have perfect visibility. In that situation, communication becomes even more important.

From Jiwon’s observations at work, Joshua communicates in a way that is fast, precise, and direct, but still warm. When Jiwon feels lost, he gives direction by explaining what he would have done, without forcing her to follow his exact answer or making her feel pressured. He gives enough structure to help people move forward, but enough openness for them to keep thinking.

03

BrandHardware identityCoherence

Joshua looks to Meter’s product line, color palette, and clean, minimal, friendly feeling to judge whether hardware belongs to the system.

Key insightA product can be minimal and still generic; coherence requires intention.

Brand identity in hardware is not only a logo or surface treatment. It is a set of decisions that build consistency over time. A product can be minimal but still cold. It can be clean but still generic. For Meter, the goal seems to be creating hardware that feels precise and reliable, but also approachable.

This is a real design challenge in the work Jiwon has observed. Networking hardware already has a strong existing visual language because of large legacy companies like Cisco. If a product becomes too generic, it risks disappearing into that category. But if it becomes too expressive, it may lose the trust and seriousness needed for enterprise infrastructure. Meter’s hardware has to feel like Meter: clean, minimal, friendly, and intentional.

In a public MeterUp 2025 hardware presentation, Joshua introduced Meter’s new enterprise networking hardware platforms, connecting the physical products to Meter’s broader hardware philosophy. This matched what we heard in the interview. The design challenge is not just to make individual objects look good. It is to make the whole system feel coherent.

04

FeedbackTeam cultureBig picture

Joshua described the team as a strong group where everyone brings one block to the work, while he tries to see the big picture and take responsibility.

Key insightUseful feedback gives direction without treating one reaction as the final answer.

Instead of describing leadership as being the person with the best idea, Joshua described it as being responsible for the bigger picture. That is different from controlling every decision. It is more about understanding how each person’s contribution fits together and making sure the work keeps moving in the right direction.

This matches what Jiwon has experienced from him. His feedback is direct, but not discouraging. When he gives feedback on new hardware concepts, he is clear about what he likes and what does not feel aligned yet. At the same time, he stays open to possibilities depending on manufacturability and feasibility. He helps the team understand the direction and the tradeoffs.

For Jiwon as an intern, this kind of feedback is very helpful. She does not always know the full hardware process, and there are many technical details that are still new to her. Joshua makes space for questions, even when they are basic. He explains things patiently when she is confused, and he makes it clear that asking questions is okay.

05

CommunicationCheck-insSafety

Joshua said clear communication is the leadership skill he relies on most, especially through quick regular check-ins rather than only formal meetings.

Key insightWith clear communication, a team can move through ambiguity together.

He explained that communication does not always have to happen through formal meetings. Quick regular check-ins help everyone understand what everyone else is doing. He also tries to be clear about what is happening with CEO conversations and how the company is growing, because the team wants the company to be successful.

This connected strongly to the weekly hardware team meetings he conducts. These meetings help Jiwon understand what everyone is working on, what new production updates are happening, and how different products are moving forward. Even when her own tasks are not directly connected to every update, hearing the bigger picture helps her understand the team’s rhythm.

Joshua can be serious, but he is also funny. That combination makes the environment feel focused without feeling cold. He communicates directly, but his general tone is warm. For Jiwon, this creates a sense of psychological safety. Before this interview, we thought of design leadership mostly in terms of vision, taste, or decision-making. After speaking with Joshua, we understood that communication may be one of the most important leadership tools.

06

Young designersCreativityConstraints

Joshua wants young designers to understand what is not feasible while still pushing creatively and finding ways to make ideas work.

Key insightGood hardware design lives between safe practicality and impossible imagination.

This answer reframed feasibility for us. We used to think feasibility meant reducing ideas or making them more practical. Joshua’s answer suggested that feasibility can also be a creative challenge. The goal is not to ignore constraints, but also not to let constraints kill the idea too early.

He connected this to a new product Meter is currently developing, which is exploring a new direction in the design language. He wants this product to become a representation of Meter. That means the team has to push the design forward while still making it manufacturable and realistic.

This is one of the hardest parts of hardware design. If a designer only focuses on what is possible today, the design may become safe and generic. If a designer ignores feasibility completely, the concept may never become real. Strong hardware design seems to live between those two extremes.

07

ProductionPatienceMilestone

Joshua said the mass production of the new series is exciting because the team has worked long and hard to make it happen.

Key insightThe final object carries the memory of sketches, prototypes, testing, suppliers, and persistence.

That answer felt simple, but it captured something meaningful about hardware. In hardware, the distance between idea and reality can be long. A product has to move through sketches, CAD, prototypes, testing, engineering, suppliers, manufacturing, and production. There are many chances for something to fail or change.

Because of that, reaching mass production is not just a business milestone. It is an emotional milestone for the team. It also made us think about patience. In school, design projects often move quickly from concept to presentation. But in a real hardware company, launching a product means dealing with many layers of reality.

The excitement comes not only from the final object, but from knowing how much work it took to make it real.

08

RitualsHumanityTrust

Joshua says good morning and goodbye to people by name. Over time, that small habit made the workplace feel more personal.

Key insightPeople trust leaders partly because leaders become legible as people.

At first, Jiwon thought this habit was kind of funny or unusual, but over time she realized it makes the workplace feel more personal. It shows that he notices people.

That detail changed how we thought about leadership. Leadership is not only shown in big decisions, presentations, or strategy meetings. It also shows up in tiny rituals that make people feel seen. Saying someone’s name is simple, but it changes the tone of the workspace. It makes the office feel less transactional and more human.

There are also small funny details that make him approachable. He does not seem to like chocolate or desserts, and when he receives sweets as gifts, he will directly say thank you but explain that he does not eat them and will share them with the team. At the same time, people on the team know that he likes beer and gummy bears. These details helped us understand that leadership is also relational.

09

Personal reflectionGrowthSystems

The interview helped us admire directness without intimidation, strong opinions without rigidity, and fast problem-solving that still leaves room for listening.

Key insightHardware leadership is responsibility to product, team, company, suppliers, budget, and customer at once.

We admire Joshua’s ability to be direct without being intimidating. He gives feedback clearly, but he does not make people feel small. He solves problems quickly, but he still listens. He has strong opinions, but he also stays open to feasibility, collaboration, and the contributions of the team.

The biggest lesson we took from him is that hardware leadership is a practice of responsibility. It requires responsibility to the product, the team, the company, the suppliers, the budget, and the customer. A leader has to care about the vision, but also about the path that makes the vision real.

As young designers, we want to become more confident in our design opinions, but also more aware of the systems around them. We want to understand manufacturing, engineering, cost, deployment, and business constraints without letting those constraints make our ideas smaller too soon. We also want to learn how to communicate more clearly, especially with people from different disciplines.

10

Closing ideaTrustReal world

Sometimes leadership looks like a quick check-in, a direct piece of feedback, a supplier call, a pricing conversation, a team meeting, or a simple good morning.

Key insightThe quiet discipline is bringing ideas into the real world without losing meaning.

Working with Joshua has shown us that creative leadership does not always look dramatic. Sometimes it looks like a quick check-in, a direct piece of feedback, a supplier call, a realistic conversation about pricing, a weekly team meeting, or a simple good morning with someone’s name.

Overall, interviewing Joshua helped us see hardware design leadership as the quiet discipline of bringing ideas into the real world. It is not only about making something beautiful. It is about making something possible, trustworthy, and meaningful enough to survive production.

03 · Design leadership synthesis

Hardware leadership is a discipline of translation.

Vision → Buildability

Strong concepts need leaders who can carry them through supplier realities, production costs, quality checks, and operational consequences.

Taste → Coherence

Hardware identity is made through repeated decisions: color, form, friendliness, restraint, reliability, and the confidence to avoid generic category language.

Authority → Responsibility

The leader does not have to contribute more than everyone else. The leader helps everyone’s contribution fit together and takes responsibility when something happens.

Communication → Psychological safety

Fast, clear, warm communication gives people enough structure to move forward and enough openness to keep thinking.

Final takeaway Hardware design leadership is not only about making something beautiful. It is about making something possible, trustworthy, and meaningful enough to survive production.
